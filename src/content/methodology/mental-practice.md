# Mental Practice

> The instrument is not required. In fact, it's often getting in the way.

## What it is

Mental practice is deliberate, effortful engagement with music **without touching the instrument**. Studying a score, hearing a passage vividly in your head, visualizing your hands executing a phrase, working out fingerings on a table — all of it counts.

The research is startling. Multiple studies (e.g., Coffman 1990; Ross 1985; Rubin-Rabson 1941) have found that mental practice produces roughly 60-75% of the improvement of physical practice for the same amount of time. Combining mental and physical practice produces *better* results than an equal amount of physical practice alone. The brain doesn't fully distinguish executing a movement from vividly imagining executing it — the same motor circuits fire.

For musicians, this unlocks:

- **Practice time in places you can't use the instrument.** Commutes, planes, waiting rooms, in bed, on long walks.
- **Memory work that's impossible with the instrument in your hands.** You can't test whether you truly know a piece from memory when the physical patterns are guiding you. Mental practice removes that crutch.
- **Injury recovery.** When physical practice isn't possible (hand injury, exhaustion, illness), mental practice keeps the material alive.
- **Deeper understanding.** You notice things in the score away from the instrument that you'd miss while playing.

## When to use it

- Preparing for performance from memory. Mental practice is the gold standard for memorization.
- Any moment you have 5+ minutes and can't touch the instrument.
- Right before you fall asleep — sleep consolidates the day's motor learning.
- During injuries or forced breaks.
- After a physical practice session — a 5-minute mental recap solidifies what you just worked on.

**Not a full replacement for physical practice.** You still need the hands. Mental practice is a multiplier, not a substitute.

## What it looks like in practice

Different modes of mental practice serve different purposes. Mix and match.

**Score study.** Open the score. Read it silently, hearing each note in your head. Track your fingers on a table or in the air. When you hit a spot you can't hear, stop — figure out what's happening theoretically (what chord, what mode, what interval) until you can hear it. This is the most rigorous mode and the highest payoff.

**Visualization.** Close your eyes. Play the piece from start to finish in your head. See your hands moving. Hear every note. When your mind wanders or a section blurs, that's diagnostic — that section isn't as solid as you thought. Note it. Work it in your next physical session.

**Aural rehearsal.** Just listen internally. Play the piece in your head without any physical motion. Slower is fine; the point is complete audibility. If a phrase is unclear in your head, it will be unclear in performance.

**Silent fingering.** Especially good for pianists / guitarists / anyone with complex fingering choices. Sit at a table, mime the fingering. Feel the shape without producing sound.

**Concept work.** Analyze the piece: what's the form, what's the key relationship between sections, where are the climaxes, what's the harmonic rhythm, what's the phrase structure. Understanding this deepens memory dramatically.

**Session structure**: pick one mode for a session; don't try to do all five at once. Twenty minutes of focused score study beats an hour of drifting between modes.

## Common mistakes

- **Not being rigorous.** Mental practice fails when you let yourself skim. If you can't clearly hear the passage internally, you don't know it — don't pretend otherwise. Slow down.
- **Doing it while distracted.** Mental practice ON a train ride works; mental practice WHILE scrolling social media doesn't. Attention is required.
- **Skipping the diagnostic step.** When mental practice reveals a fuzzy spot, that's data. Ignoring it defeats the purpose. Write it down for tomorrow.
- **Only doing it in emergencies.** Musicians tend to remember mental practice when they can't use the instrument. Making it a regular part of the routine yields much more.
- **Trying too hard on visualization.** Vivid mental imagery gets easier with practice. If your first attempts are dim, that's normal. Keep going.

## Further reading

- Grace Rubin-Rabson, "Studies in the psychology of memorizing piano music: II. A comparison of massed and distributed practice" (1941) — first serious research on mental practice for musicians.
- Don Coffman, "Effects of mental practice, physical practice, and knowledge of results on piano performance" (1990) — the numbers on mental vs physical practice ratios.
- Susan Ross, "The effectiveness of mental practice in improving the performance of college trombonists" (1985) — clean experimental design confirming the effect for wind players.
- Molly Gebrian, *Learn Faster, Perform Better* (2024) — Chapter 5 has excellent practical protocols.
