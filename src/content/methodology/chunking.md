# Chunking

> A piece is not a piece to your brain. It's a sequence of chunks that link to each other.

## What it is

When memory researchers study how expert musicians recall repertoire, they consistently find the music is stored as **chunks** — meaningful segments of 2–8 measures that group naturally by phrase structure. Beginners try to memorize the whole thing top-to-bottom. Experts learn one chunk at a time, then learn the transitions between chunks separately.

Chunking is the practice discipline that mirrors this cognitive reality. Instead of playing through the piece over and over hoping problem spots will resolve, you:

1. Identify the natural chunks.
2. Master each chunk in isolation.
3. Master each **transition** between chunks in isolation.
4. Then re-assemble.

The transitions are the key insight. Most passages we can play "when we get there." What we can't play is the *arrival at* the passage from wherever we just came from. Isolating the transition — the last note of chunk N through the first four notes of chunk N+1 — is where most of the practice time actually goes.

## When to use it

- You're learning a new piece.
- You're polishing a piece you can play but with frequent flubs at the same spots.
- A specific spot in a piece won't come clean, and you keep re-starting from the top hoping it will.
- Any music-reading work: sight-reading, orchestral parts, session charts. All benefit from chunking.

**Not for**: sight-reading fresh material for the first time (that's a different skill), improvised playing (there aren't discrete chunks to isolate).

## What it looks like in practice

**1. Map the piece.** Pull out the score (or your ear-transcribed sketch). Draw brackets around natural chunks: usually 4–8 measures, ending on a phrase boundary. A 32-bar tune is typically 4 chunks of 8 measures each. Number them: A1, A2, B, A3 (for AABA form), or just Chunk 1 through Chunk 8.

**2. List the transitions.** Between every consecutive pair of chunks is a transition. For 8 chunks, that's 7 transitions. Number these too: T1-2 (between chunk 1 and 2), T2-3, etc.

**3. Baseline each chunk.** Play each chunk in isolation, straight through. Mark which chunks are already clean and which need work. Prioritize the chunks that flub the most.

**4. Work each priority chunk deliberately.** Apply Slow Practice (see that article) to each rough chunk. When a chunk plays clean at target tempo 5 times in a row, mark it done.

**5. Now work the transitions.** Play only the last measure of chunk N + the first measure of chunk N+1. This is a very short passage — maybe 4-8 beats. Loop it. Get it clean. Move to the next transition.

**6. Assemble incrementally.** Play chunk 1 + chunk 2 (with the transition). Then chunks 1 + 2 + 3. Then 1 + 2 + 3 + 4. Every time you add a chunk, if it breaks, drop back and work that transition specifically.

**7. Play the whole thing.** Only after the chunks + transitions are all clean. If it still breaks in a specific spot, that spot is a chunk-boundary you missed — add it to your transition list and work it.

## Common mistakes

- **Chunks too big.** If a chunk is 16 measures long, it's not a chunk — it's a section. Break it down.
- **Chunks too small.** Chunking every measure separately loses the phrase structure. The chunks should feel like natural units when you play them.
- **Skipping transitions.** This is the most common failure mode. Musicians master the chunks in isolation and assume the whole will work. It won't; the transitions need equal time.
- **Playing from the top when it breaks.** If you break in bar 22, don't restart at bar 1. Isolate the transition where it broke. Re-starting from the top is the enemy of chunking.
- **Assembling too fast.** Adding two chunks at a time (jump straight from 1+2 to 3+4+5+6) skips the incremental buildup that makes the transitions stable. One chunk at a time.

## Further reading

- Herbert A. Simon & William G. Chase, "Skill in Chess" (1973) — original research on chunking as a memory mechanism (chess, but the model transfers).
- Anders Ericsson, *Peak* (2016) — Chapter 4 covers chunking in expert performance.
- Barbara Lister-Sink, *Freeing the Caged Bird* (2014) — pianist-focused chunking work with excellent practical drills.
- Gerald Klickstein, *The Musician's Way* (2009) — Chapter 5 has the definitive musician-focused walkthrough of the method.
