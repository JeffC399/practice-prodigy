# Spaced Repetition

> Practicing something once a day beats practicing it seven times on Sunday.

## What it is

Hermann Ebbinghaus discovered the "forgetting curve" in 1885: memory decays predictably after learning, and re-exposure at the right moment restores it — with each re-exposure lasting longer than the last.

The practical consequence: to remember something forever, you don't need to practice it forever. You need to practice it at **increasing intervals**. Day 1, then day 2, then day 4, then day 8, then day 16, then day 32, and eventually the material becomes essentially permanent with occasional touch-ups.

Language learners popularized the technique (Anki flashcards, Duolingo). Music applies the same principle: any material you want in your long-term hands (scales, standards, licks, chord voicings, ear-training patterns) benefits enormously from spaced review versus block practice.

## When to use it

- **Warmups and technique fundamentals** — scales, arpeggios, patterns you want at your fingertips forever.
- **Repertoire maintenance** — tunes you've learned but don't want to forget.
- **Ear-training vocabulary** — intervals, chord qualities, cadential patterns.
- **Any lick, voicing, or shape** you want to have "in the hands" without thinking.

**Not for**: material you're actively learning from scratch (that needs concentrated blocked time first). Spaced repetition is for the maintenance phase after initial acquisition.

## What it looks like in practice

**1. Build a review queue.** Every item you've learned goes into a queue: "12 major scales," "ii-V-I in every key," "20 jazz standards I've committed to memory," "melodic minor modes across the neck."

**2. Assign each item to an interval bucket.** Beginners' bucket schedule:

- Bucket 1: review daily
- Bucket 2: review every 2 days
- Bucket 3: review every 4 days
- Bucket 4: review every 8 days
- Bucket 5: review every 16 days
- Bucket 6: review every 32 days
- Bucket 7: review monthly (mature)

**3. When you practice an item, evaluate it.** If you nail it, promote it to the next bucket (longer interval). If you flub it, demote back to bucket 1 (daily review) until it's solid again.

**4. Each session, only touch the items whose intervals have come due.** A properly-tuned queue means each session covers a mix: some daily items you're building, some mature items you're touching, no items you're wasting time on.

**5. Add new material to the queue at bucket 1.** Learned a new tune? Put it in bucket 1. Every day for a week, then every other day, then every four days. In a month, you've barely thought about it but you can still play it. In three months, it's yours.

**6. Retire mature items** (bucket 7+) after 6 months of clean reviews. Or keep them in a "hall of fame" that gets touched quarterly.

## The lightweight version

Full spaced-repetition tracking is admin-heavy. A lighter approach that captures 80% of the benefit:

- Every warmup, cycle through a rotating subset of your technique library. Ten items per warmup, drawn from a pool of 50, means each item gets touched every 5 days.
- Every repertoire session, refresh one "old" tune before working on new material.
- Rotate ear-training sets: hard intervals Monday, chord qualities Tuesday, cadential patterns Wednesday. Never the same content two days in a row.

This is not textbook spaced repetition — but it's the version most musicians actually stick with, and it produces most of the benefit.

## Common mistakes

- **Reviewing too often.** Playing the same warmup every day forever isn't spaced repetition — it's blocked repetition. The whole point is spreading exposures out.
- **Reviewing too rarely.** If you forget an item completely between exposures, the interval was too long. Move it back to a shorter interval.
- **No feedback loop.** If you never track which items are solid vs shaky, the system collapses to "review whatever comes to mind." That's not spaced repetition either.
- **Over-engineering the tracking.** The full Anki-style tracking system is overkill for most musicians. Start with the lightweight version.
- **Forgetting that new material still needs concentrated learning.** Spaced repetition is for the *maintenance* phase. Don't try to learn a new tune via spaced repetition alone.
