# Interleaved Practice

> The counter-intuitive one. It feels worse. It works better.

## What it is

Most of us practice in blocks: "OK, 30 minutes of arpeggios, then 30 minutes of the tune, then 30 minutes of ear training." That's called **blocked practice**. It feels productive because you get warmed up on each skill and progress quickly during the block.

**Interleaved practice** does the opposite. It rotates between skills every few minutes: two minutes of arpeggios, then two minutes of the tune, then two minutes of ear training, then back to arpeggios. Never long enough to settle in.

Here's the thing: cognitive-science research on skill acquisition (Rohrer, Taylor, and others) has consistently found that interleaved practice produces **worse in-session performance** and **substantially better long-term retention** than blocked practice. Musicians who interleaved played worse at the end of the session but demonstrably better a week later — on the same material.

The mechanism: switching between skills forces your brain to reconstruct each skill's context every time you return to it. That reconstruction is where lasting learning happens. Blocked practice lets you coast on the just-set-up context; interleaved makes you rebuild it every couple minutes.

## When to use it

- You're learning multiple related skills and want them all to stick.
- You've noticed that you can nail something in the practice room but flub it live — interleaved practice trains for "cold" performance.
- You're bored with blocked practice and want variety within a session.
- Late-stage learning: after you can already do each skill in isolation.

**Not for**: initial acquisition of a brand-new skill. When you're first learning something, you need blocked time to establish the fingering, the shape, the pattern. Once it's basically there, switch to interleaved to make it stick.

## What it looks like in practice

**1. Pick 3–5 skills** you want to work on. They can be related (three arpeggio patterns, three keys of a scale) or unrelated (arpeggios, sight-reading, ear training). Both work.

**2. Set a rotation length.** Shorter = harder = better retention. Longer = easier = more like blocked. Start with 3–5 minutes per skill; work down to 2 minutes as you build the mental switching muscle.

**3. Rotate.** Timer-driven. When the timer chimes, stop mid-attempt if you have to and move to the next skill. This will feel wrong. Do it anyway.

**4. Log what happened.** During the transition, spend 15 seconds noting where you got to and what you'd try next time. This makes the reconstruction easier when you come back.

**5. Repeat the rotation** for the full session. A 60-minute interleaved session with 5 skills at 3 min each = 4 full rotations. Each skill gets 12 minutes total, but chopped into 4 encounters.

**6. Don't judge in-session progress.** You will feel like you're not improving. Trust the research. Come back tomorrow.

## Common mistakes

- **Rotating too infrequently.** 15-minute chunks isn't interleaved practice — that's just a schedule. The magic happens with 2–5 minute chunks.
- **Skipping a skill because it's "not warmed up yet."** That's the point. Cold-start each skill; that's the practice.
- **Mixing skills that don't share a purpose.** You want the skills related enough that reconstruction transfers. Ten unrelated skills is chaos; three related skills is interleaved practice.
- **Giving up after one session.** The whole benefit shows up between sessions, not within them. You have to string 3–4 interleaved sessions together to see the retention win.
