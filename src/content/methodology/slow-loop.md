# The Slow Loop

> When Slow Practice + Chunking meet a metronome and refuse to let go.

## What it is

Slow Loop is a surgical variant of Slow Practice widely used in jazz education (Barry Harris pedagogy, the Berklee tradition, countless private teachers). The idea: pick the ONE hardest measure or 2-4 beat lick that's tripping you up, loop it at a very slow tempo for a fixed number of clean repetitions, then nudge the tempo up and repeat. Continue until you're at target tempo — or until you plateau, at which point you leave the plateau tempo saved for tomorrow.

Where Slow Practice covers whole passages, Slow Loop cuts down to the smallest possible unit. Where Chunking assembles pieces, Slow Loop drills the trouble spots inside chunks. It's the workshop tool you reach for when everything else has narrowed the problem down to "this specific bar."

## When to use it

- You've isolated a specific hard bar / lick / transition and just need to burn it in.
- You have 10-15 minutes and want visible measurable progress on one tiny thing.
- A passage that everything else has failed to fix.
- Improvised vocabulary: internalizing a specific ii-V lick or a chromatic approach pattern until it's automatic.

**Not for**: material that's not yet identified as your problem spot. If you don't know exactly what's failing, do Slow Practice on the whole passage first to surface the target, then Slow Loop the target.

## What it looks like in practice

**1. Isolate the exact loop.** Not the whole bar; the exact notes that fail. Often it's 4-8 notes, sometimes just 2 pitches with a difficult interval between them. Write down what you're looping.

**2. Find the tempo where it's 90% clean.** Start at a tempo that feels absurdly slow. Play the loop 10 times in a row. How many were clean (every note, right pitch, right articulation, no tension)?

- If 9-10 clean → you're at your starting tempo. Move on.
- If 6-8 clean → drop 20% and re-baseline.
- If <6 clean → drop 30% and re-baseline.

The starting tempo is the fastest tempo at which you get 90% clean.

**3. Do "the count" at that tempo.** Standard count is **5 clean in a row**. That means five consecutive clean plays with no flubs. If you flub #3, restart the count at zero. You're not done until you've hit 5 straight.

Some traditions use 10 clean in a row for harder material. Choose based on how important the passage is; 5 is enough for most work.

**4. Nudge up 5 BPM.** Do the count again at the new tempo. If you can't hit 5 straight, drop back to the last tempo where you did — and stay there for another count of 5. Then try nudging again.

**5. Repeat until you hit your target tempo** or plateau. If you plateau at 100 BPM but the target is 140, that's fine — you've made real progress. Save 100 as tomorrow's starting point.

**6. Leave the loop alone.** When you close the session, don't play the loop at full tempo just to "see if you can." That's fishing for validation, not building the skill. Trust the slow work; return tomorrow.

## Why "5 clean in a row"?

The count matters because it prevents the illusion of mastery. Playing something cleanly once, then once more, then flubbing, then getting it — feels good but doesn't prove you can execute reliably. Five consecutive clean plays with no reset proves reliability. Under stage stress, reliable execution is what you have; anything less falls apart.

Some players prefer "3 clean in a row" — that's fine, just less strict. Others use "10 clean in a row" for material they'll perform. Pick a count you'll actually enforce.

## Common mistakes

- **Not restarting the count when you flub.** If you flubbed on rep 4, the count is not "4 out of 5" — it's "0 out of 5, try again." Sloppy counting = sloppy work.
- **Being lenient about "clean."** A note that's a little late is not clean. A tone that's a little tense is not clean. Strictness is the whole method.
- **Speeding up too fast.** 5 BPM increments. Not 15. Not 20.
- **Skipping the isolation step.** If your loop is "the whole 8-bar section," it's not Slow Loop — it's just Slow Practice on a section. Slow Loop is measured in beats, not measures.
- **Playing it at full tempo to "test" mid-session.** This resets your loose motion + tension habits. Don't. Wait for tomorrow.
