# Deliberate Practice

> Not just "practicing more." Practicing what you can't do — and knowing whether you did it.

## What it is

Anders Ericsson's framework for how people actually get good at hard things. Airline pilots, chess grandmasters, world-class violinists — Ericsson interviewed the best of the best across decades of research and found they had one thing in common. It wasn't talent. It wasn't hours. It was **how** they spent those hours.

Deliberate practice has four ingredients:

1. **A specific, measurable goal** for the session. Not "practice arpeggios." Specifically: "play the A♭ minor 7 arpeggio at 80 BPM, four notes per beat, cleanly, ten times in a row."
2. **Full concentration.** Not with the radio on. Not while checking your phone. The frontier of your ability is fragile.
3. **Immediate feedback.** You know within seconds whether that attempt was clean or not. If you can't tell, you're not practicing deliberately — you're just playing.
4. **Deliberate discomfort.** The work sits just past what you can already do. Not so far past that you flub every attempt (frustrating). Not so easy that you nail it every time (wasted reps). Roughly: you should succeed 30-70% of the time.

The critical insight: playing something you can already play well is not practice. It's performance. Performance is fine — you should perform. But if the goal is to improve, you need to spend focused time in the zone where you can barely do the thing.

## When to use it

- You've hit a plateau — the same problem keeps showing up in the same passage.
- You have a specific technical weakness you can name in one sentence.
- You have limited time (30–60 min) and want maximum improvement per minute.
- You're preparing for a specific gig / recital / lesson and you know what will trip you up.

**Not for**: warm-ups, cool-downs, sight-reading fresh material, exploratory jamming. Those are all valuable, just different animals.

## What it looks like in practice

**1. Warm up separately.** Deliberate practice is high-effort; you can't do it cold. Spend 5–10 minutes getting loose. Don't count this as your practice time.

**2. Identify today's target.** One thing. Not two. Not a general area. One specific pattern, passage, transition, phrase, chord, interval, whatever. Write it down. "Left hand independence on the third measure of the B section." "Clean 16ths across the neck at 100 BPM." "The interval jump from bar 12 to bar 13."

**3. Baseline it.** Play it as it is right now. How many attempts out of ten come out clean? If it's 8+, this isn't your challenge zone — pick something harder. If it's 0–2, back off — pick something easier. You want 3–7 out of 10.

**4. Work on it for 20-30 minutes.** Slow it down. Isolate the smallest chunk that's still failing. Loop it. Vary the rhythm. Vary the articulation. Play it in a different key. Play only the difficult transition, not the surrounding passage. Every attempt, ask yourself: was that clean? If not, what specifically went wrong? Fix that one thing next attempt.

**5. Rest.** Full stop. Walk away. Get water. Come back in 3–5 minutes. Your brain consolidates during rest — this isn't downtime.

**6. Contextualize.** Play the target in its full context. If it was a passage from a piece, play the whole section. If it was a technical pattern, weave it into some improvisation. Does it hold up when the surrounding music is asking things of you? Often it doesn't — that tells you what to work on next session.

**7. Reflect.** Two minutes at the end. What worked? What didn't? What's tomorrow's target — same thing again, or a different weakness?

## Common mistakes

- **Practicing what you can already play.** It feels productive. It isn't. You're maintaining, not improving.
- **Trying to fix too many things at once.** One target per session. Two if you must. Not eight.
- **Skipping the rest.** Deliberate practice is metabolically expensive; your brain can only sustain it for 20-30 minutes at a stretch. Push through and you're just doing sloppy reps.
- **Forgetting to baseline + measure.** If you can't say "I got 3 out of 10 clean today" and then "I got 7 out of 10 clean by Friday," you can't tell whether the work is working.
- **Being too kind to yourself about "clean."** If you have to squint to call an attempt clean, it wasn't. Honesty about failure is what makes the method work.
