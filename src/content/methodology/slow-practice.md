# Slow Practice

> If you can't play it slowly and cleanly, you can't play it fast. You're just playing it fast badly.

## What it is

Slow practice is exactly what it sounds like: playing a passage substantially below its target tempo, focusing on accuracy, tone, and physical relaxation. Then gradually speeding up.

The idea is ancient — every serious teacher has been saying it for centuries. But there's a real neurological reason it works. When you play slowly, you have time to notice: which finger is tensing up, which note is landing early, which chord change is fudged. Fast playing hides all of that. Slow playing exposes it. And once exposed, you can fix it.

Speed then becomes a matter of removing the wasted motion + tension you found during slow work. Not a matter of "trying harder."

## When to use it

- Any technique you're trying to speed up. Scales, arpeggios, licks, orchestral passages, everything.
- Learning a new piece — start way under tempo before you attempt performance speed.
- Correcting sloppy playing you've noticed at fast tempos.
- Warming up cold hands. Slow tempo lets your muscles wake up properly.

**Slow practice is nearly universally applicable.** Almost everything you play benefits from time spent slow. The only exception: some music (fast improvisation, groove-based playing) has a *feel* that only exists at tempo, and slow practice can't teach that. But the technical vocabulary underneath the feel — that still comes from slow work.

## What it looks like in practice

**1. Pick a target tempo.** The tempo you want to play the passage AT eventually. Write it down.

**2. Find your "clean tempo."** Start at the target tempo minus 30%. Play the passage. Is it clean — every note the right pitch, right rhythm, right articulation, hands relaxed? If yes, you found your clean tempo. If no, drop another 10% and try again. Keep dropping until you're clean.

For beginners on hard material, clean tempo might be 40% of the target. That's fine.

**3. Play it 3-5 times at clean tempo.** Full concentration. If any attempt is not clean, drop the tempo further and start over.

**4. Nudge up 3-5 BPM.** Play it 3-5 times. Still clean? Keep going up. First flub — drop back to the last clean tempo and stay there for a few more reps.

**5. When you plateau, stop.** Don't push through with sloppiness. Save the plateau tempo somewhere (in the drill notes, on a sticky, whatever). Tomorrow's session starts one BPM below there.

**6. Over multiple sessions**, the plateau tempo creeps up. That's the whole game.

**Optional advanced move: alternate slow + target.** Play the passage at clean tempo, then immediately at target tempo, then back to clean, then target. The contrast is very informative. What you couldn't do at target becomes obvious.

## Common mistakes

- **Not slow enough.** Musicians consistently underestimate how slow "slow" needs to be. If you can play it fast without effort, slow is 50-60% of that. If you're struggling, slow might be 30-40%.
- **Speeding up too fast.** 3-5 BPM at a time. Not 20 BPM at a time. The whole point is incremental.
- **Being impatient.** You want to see progress in one session. Slow practice pays off across many sessions.
- **Neglecting to record where you got to.** Tomorrow you should start below yesterday's plateau, not at your target tempo. Track the number.
- **Practicing sloppily at ANY tempo.** The whole method depends on the "clean" measurement. If you're calling messy playing clean, the method breaks.
