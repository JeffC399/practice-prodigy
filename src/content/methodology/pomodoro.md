# Pomodoro Practice

> A structure, not a philosophy. Use when you need one.

## What it is

Francesco Cirillo invented the Pomodoro Technique in the 1980s for office work: 25 minutes of focused work, 5 minutes of rest, repeat. After four "pomodoros" (25-min blocks), a longer 15–30 minute break.

The name comes from the tomato-shaped kitchen timer Cirillo used to time his blocks. That's the whole system.

Music practice benefits from it in specific ways:

- **Attention is finite.** Most players lose real focus after 20-30 minutes. Structural breaks harvest what you have and prevent the sloppy-reps zone.
- **Rest is when consolidation happens.** Research on motor learning consistently finds that 5-minute breaks between practice blocks improve retention more than 5 extra minutes of continuous practice.
- **It creates urgency.** Knowing you have 25 minutes and no more focuses the work. "I'll get to it in this session" doesn't happen — you commit or you don't.
- **It bounds procrastination.** Starting a 90-minute session feels heavy. Starting a 25-minute one is easy. You often do more than one pomodoro because starting was the hard part.

## When to use it

- You have limited time (45–120 min) and want to use it well.
- You've been drifting during long sessions and want structure to snap you back.
- You're combining practice with other work (composition, transcription, listening) and want clean context switches.
- Your practice sessions have been feeling exhausting — the built-in rests are the fix.

**Not for**: sessions shorter than 30 minutes (just practice, no timer needed), or when you're deep in a groove and pausing would break the moment (jam sessions, improvisation flow states, performance rehearsal).

## What it looks like in practice

**1. Decide how many pomodoros** you're going to do. Two is a 55-min session (25 + 5 + 25). Three is 80 min. Four is 110 min. Rarely go beyond four in one sitting.

**2. Pick what each pomodoro will contain.** You don't have to switch tasks between pomodoros — you can spend all four on the same piece. Or you can dedicate each to a different focus (Technique / Repertoire / Ear Training / Sight Reading). The structure is agnostic to content.

**3. Start the first pomodoro.** 25 minutes. Full concentration. No phone. If your mind wanders, note where it went and bring it back. If someone interrupts, say "give me 20 minutes" if possible.

**4. Stop when the timer chimes.** Even mid-attempt. Even if you're on the verge of a breakthrough. Interrupting mid-flow feels wrong — it's meant to. The 5-minute rest is when your brain does the actual encoding.

**5. Rest for 5 minutes.** Stand up. Walk. Drink water. Look at something far away. **Do not check your phone** — that's not rest, that's a different form of attention. The rest works because it's genuinely restful.

**6. Start the next pomodoro.** Repeat.

**7. After 4 pomodoros, take a longer break** (15–30 min). Real food. Real conversation. Then decide whether to start another set or wrap up.

## Common mistakes

- **Extending pomodoros to "finish."** Don't. 25 minutes is 25 minutes. Whatever you didn't finish is tomorrow's problem. Extending erodes the whole system.
- **Skipping rests to save time.** The rests aren't optional. They're what makes it work. Session with rest > longer session without rest.
- **Using rest for other work.** Cognitive rest means not thinking hard. Reading email, texting, browsing — all forms of thinking hard.
- **Trying to Pomodoro a 15-minute session.** Overkill. Just practice.
- **Not committing to full attention during the block.** If a Pomodoro is a Pomodoro in name only (background music, occasional glance at phone), you're just watching a timer. Do the practice or don't.

## Further reading

- Francesco Cirillo, *The Pomodoro Technique* (2006) — original short book. Very short. Worth reading.
- Cal Newport, *Deep Work* (2016) — the broader case for focused attention blocks (not music-specific but the mechanisms are the same).
- Molly Gebrian, *Learn Faster, Perform Better* (2024) — Chapter 6 has research-backed practice-length recommendations, all of which align with Pomodoro-style breaks.
